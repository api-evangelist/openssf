	<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">By </span>Jonas Rosland</p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Security teams in 2026 have no shortage of data, alerts, or findings. In 2025 alone, </span><a href="https://jerrygamblin.com/2026/01/01/2025-cve-data-review/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">48,185 Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVEs)</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> were published, a 20.6% increase over 2024&#8217;s already record-breaking total of 39,962. That works out to roughly 130 new vulnerabilities disclosed every single day, and for seven consecutive years, the annual count has hit a new record high.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The drivers are structural: the explosive growth of open source software, the complexity of transitive dependencies hidden deep in software supply chains, and an expanding CVE ecosystem that now encompasses </span><a href="https://medium.com/s2wblog/detailed-analysis-of-recent-vulnerability-trends-and-attack-patterns-742d37ff4ced"><span style="font-weight: 400;">nearly twice as many reporting organizations</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> as it did five years ago. With </span><a href="https://www.sysdig.com/press-releases/sysdig-2023-usage-report"><span style="font-weight: 400;">97% of commercial applications</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> containing open source components, inherited risk has become a routine part of working with modern software.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">While only </span><a href="https://www.edgescan.com/stats-report/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">2% of all discovered vulnerabilities</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> are ever exploited in the wild, of that small fraction, </span><a href="https://www.vulncheck.com/blog/state-of-exploitation-2026"><span style="font-weight: 400;">nearly 29% were exploited</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> on or </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">before</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> the day their CVE was published. Attackers are selective, but once they identify a target, the window for defenders is very narrow. The window between vulnerability disclosure and confirmed exploitation is also shrinking. Whereas that timeline was over a year in 2020, it’s now shrunk to just </span><a href="https://zerodayclock.com/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">hours</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The old model of scanning everything, triaging by Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) score, and working through a queue simply cannot keep pace with this reality. Something has to change.</span></p>
<h2><b>Most vulnerabilities will never be exploited</b></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The vast majority of what your vulnerability scanner finds will never actually be used against you. That means the core challenge facing security teams isn&#8217;t patching speed, but knowing where to focus. When a scanner returns thousands of findings ranked only by CVSS score, what looks like a workload problem is really a prioritization problem. Critical vulnerabilities in libraries that aren&#8217;t loaded at runtime, or in containers that haven&#8217;t run in months, crowd out the findings that genuinely matter, such as exploitable vulnerabilities in running, exposed workloads. The result is alert fatigue, missed priorities, and growing friction between security and development teams.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The </span><a href="https://www.bestpractices.dev/en/criteria"><span style="font-weight: 400;">OpenSSF Best Practices criteria</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> reflect this directly. At the “Passing” level, projects must not contain unpatched vulnerabilities of medium or higher severity that have been known publicly for more than 60 days, and critical vulnerabilities should be fixed rapidly after they are reported. The emphasis here isn&#8217;t on the volume of findings processed, but on the speed and accuracy with which the most dangerous vulnerabilities are addressed, a distinction that gets lost when teams are buried in undifferentiated backlogs.</span></p>
<h2><b>Why static analysis alone isn&#8217;t enough</b></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Static analysis is non-negotiable. The OpenSSF Best Practices criteria require it at the “Passing” level, and at “Silver,” projects must use tools that look for real vulnerabilities in code, not just style issues. Integrated into CI/CD pipelines, static analysis catches bugs early when they are cheapest to fix, and it remains a solid foundation of any security program. However, alone, it’s not enough.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The limitation is that static analysis sees everything, regardless of whether it matters in practice. It cannot tell you whether a vulnerable library is actually loaded in a running container, or whether that container ever receives external traffic. A CVSS 9.8 score looks identical whether the package is called thousands of times a day in a critical service or has never once been invoked in production. Runtime security fills that gap by observing what is actually executing in production. By tracking which processes are running, which packages are loaded, and which connections are being made, security teams gain much more precise intelligence about where risk actually lives.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Only 15% of critical and high-severity vulnerabilities with an available fix are in packages actually loaded at runtime. By isolating that subset, teams can reduce the scope of what needs immediate attention to a small fraction of their total backlog, in some cases by </span><a href="https://www.sysdig.com/blog/smarter-vulnerability-management-with-in-use-prioritization"><span style="font-weight: 400;">over 95%</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. That&#8217;s the practical difference between a list that overwhelms a development team and one they can actually act on. Static analysis provides breadth by catching everything possible during development, while runtime intelligence adds depth by showing what genuinely matters in production. Together, they give teams the context to make better decisions.</span></p>
<h2><b>Helping security and development teams speak the same language</b></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Runtime data also changes how security teams and developers talk to each other. Telling a developer &#8220;this CVE is rated 8.1&#8221; lands very differently than &#8220;this vulnerability is in a package actively loaded in your production authentication service.&#8221; The second statement connects a finding to a tangible business risk, and that context helps developers understand urgency in a way that a severity score on its own rarely does.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When security teams can bring developers a short, contextualized list of what needs attention and why, the conversation tends to shift from friction to collaboration. The OpenSSF Best Practices framework supports this kind of working relationship structurally, requiring documented vulnerability response processes, response times under 14 days, and release notes that explicitly identify runtime vulnerabilities fixed in each release. These aren&#8217;t bureaucratic requirements, but the scaffolding for the kind of consistent, trust-based communication that makes vulnerability management work in practice.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Neither team can do this work alone. Security engineers don&#8217;t always know which code paths are business-critical, and developers don&#8217;t always have visibility into what their software looks like from an attacker&#8217;s perspective. Runtime data helps bridge that gap by giving both sides a shared, evidence-based view of where the real risk lives.</span></p>
<h2><b>Shrinking the problem over time</b></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Prioritization manages the vulnerability problem today, but reducing the attack surface is how you make the problem smaller tomorrow. Runtime intelligence supports two practical strategies that static scanning alone cannot.</span></p>
<ol>
<li><b> Build leaner, more deliberate images.</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Runtime analysis identifies unused packages, old utilities, and bundled libraries that never get called in production, giving teams a clear basis for stripping images down. Building from scratch or distroless base images takes this further by removing shells, package managers, and other components that have no place in a production workload, and combining that with </span><a href="https://kubernetes.web.cern.ch/blog/2025/06/19/rootless-container-builds-on-kubernetes/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">rootless containers</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> limits the damage an attacker can do if they do gain access. Runtime data can also flag containers running on stale base images that are actively receiving traffic, making the case for a refresh concrete rather than a task that keeps getting deprioritized.</span></li>
<li><b> Detect and respond to unexpected behavior in production.</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Even with good prioritization, not every risk can be patched immediately. This is where a runtime threat detection tool like Falco becomes valuable. By defining what normal behavior looks like for a given workload, Falco can flag unexpected activity in real time, such as a process spawning a shell, a container writing to a sensitive path, or an unusual outbound connection appearing. This doesn&#8217;t replace patching, but it provides a meaningful layer of protection while remediation work is underway, and it gives teams better visibility into whether a vulnerability is being actively probed or exploited.</span></li>
</ol>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The OpenSSF Best Practices criteria encourage minimizing the attack surface throughout, and the logic applies equally in production environments. The best vulnerability is the one that doesn&#8217;t exist because the vulnerable component was never there, and the next best outcome is knowing quickly when something unexpected is happening around the ones that remain.</span></p>
<h2><b>Where to go from here</b></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The 2025 numbers make one thing very clear: the volume of vulnerabilities isn&#8217;t going down, and teams that try to treat every finding with equal urgency will continue to struggle. The more practical path is to use static analysis and runtime intelligence together, letting each do what it does best, and to use that shared context to build better working relationships between security and development teams. Finding the right vulnerabilities to fix, explaining why they matter, and making it straightforward for developers to act on them is where the real progress happens.</span></p>
